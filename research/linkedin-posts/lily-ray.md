## **Post 1 — Half of SEO/GEO is knowing how NOT to destroy visibility**

Half of SEO/GEO is knowing how to improve organic visibility.  
  
The other half is knowing how to not put your site/brand in a situation where your organic visibility can be destroyed a few months later.  
  
In my opinion, it's the job of every qualified SEO/GEO professional to fully understand both sides of the equation.  
  
Many new folks are entering the space right now because AI tools make it easier than ever to "roleplay as an SEO/GEO engineer" and find some type of SEO hack that can be automated and scaled.   
  
I see the viral posts daily across many platforms: the idea that you don't need human SEOs anymore because Claude or Gemini can do all your research, content generation, content refreshes, etc. for you. (Just don't forget to like & subscribe, comment "GEO," and buy the course to learn how! 😉 )  
  
I see new companies popping up that are literally raising millions in venture capital funding making similar promises. (I feel bad for the investors.)  
  
We're back in SEO/GEO gold rush mode. We have been here many times before 🫠   
  
What many of the folks making big promises are lacking is the necessary experience in the SEO industry to know what happens on the other side. The things that happen a few months after these approaches are implemented... and after search engines have had enough time to figure out what the site is up to.   
  
Believe it or not, one of the main culprits that caused sites to get dinged over the last few years was using an overly-aggressive SEO strategy. The 2023 Helpful Content Update was designed to demote sites that were deemed as being created primarily for SEO, not for humans.  
  
I worked with many impacted companies, and know that many of them would have approached things differently if they had understood the risks. The same is true for many prior algorithm updates.  
  
This is why this has been a recurring theme in many of my conference talks over the years: because this cycle has continued for all of SEO history.   
  
We're starting to see the early signs of search engines cracking down on some of the new tricks people are exploring; but I think we are only just getting started. My money is on a major shakeup in the next few months that will do a lot of damage.  
  
My advice is: don't put your site in a situation that is incredibly difficult to dig yourself out of. It's not fun. Recoveries are extremely difficult, can take months or years to accomplish, and are not 100% guaranteed.  
  
Don't abandon or ignore your SEO strategy because of GEO. The two things should support one another.  
  
Read the search engines' spam and content policies. Work with real SEO experts who have dealt with algorithm updates and penalties before. If a tactic feels too good to be true, it probably is.

## **Post 3 — Winners & losers of February Discover Core Update**

Who were the winners & losers of the February Discover Core Update?   
  
I got some early data from Scott McNeal at DiscoverSnoop, one of the tools we use for our Google Discover audits at [Amsive](https://www.linkedin.com/company/amsive/). DiscoverSnoop will be publishing the full study with their methodology tomorrow (I'll update this post with a link when it's live.)   
  
This was the first ever official Google Discover core update, and many of us who focus on Google Discover have been curious to see what Google aimed to do with this change.  
  
However - Google Discover is extremely difficult to analyze - much more so than regular Search. Discover feeds are highly personalized, performance is inherently volatile, and 3rd party data isn't 100% accurate.   
  
That said, it's interesting to look at some of the outcomes of this update, based on DiscoverSnoop's findings.   
  
The biggest trends I see in the below data:  
  
- Several Fox franchise sites saw declines: Fox News, Fox Business, Fox Weather, and Fox5NY all landed in the top 6 hardest-hit publishers.   
  
- It wasn't just Fox - seems like various local news publications (news12, syracuse, nj, etc.) saw big declines in Discover during this update. DiscoverSnoop (which allows for state-based analysis) interpreted this as "local publishers [losing] their national reach and now mainly surfacing in their home regions."  
  
- Yahoo saw a big drop (-47%), and I've also observed less syndicated content in Discover in the last few months in general. Syndicated content has been a BIG problem in Discover for years, so it's possible Google is finally taking steps to show more original articles instead of syndicated versions.  
  
- [X.com](http://X.com) also saw a big decline, which is interesting, given I started seeing a lot more X posts on my Discover feed in the last few months.   
  
- Personally, I don't see [geediting.com](http://geediting.com) maintaining its gains for very long in Discover 🤐 And I'm a bit surprised (and mildly concerned) that this site is the biggest winner of the update...  
  
Looking forward to reading the full article tomorrow, and I'll circle back with the link when it's live!



## **Post 4 — ChatGPT 5.4 update and trusted source filtering**

This is *very* interesting.  
  
The ChatGPT 5.4 update has definitely implemented some changes that are impacting AI search.   
  
When [Chris Long](https://www.linkedin.com/in/chris-long-marketing/) and others pointed out the fan-out query changes in 5.4, my first thought was that it looks like OpenAI is introducing new systems to try to reduce citations from biased/untrustworthy sources.   
  
By conducting site: searches and limiting web searches to trusted domains (G2, Clutch), ChatGPT is retrieving a more refined list of candidates to consider for its search-enabled responses.   
  
I also thought it was interesting that when Chris looked for SEO agencies, ChatGPT's fan-out queries included names of agencies like ours ([Amsive](https://www.linkedin.com/company/amsive/)), iPullRank, Seer Interactive, Rise at Seven, and other SEO agencies I would argue have been among the leading agencies in the industry for a long time (in terms of recognition, awards, thought leadership, conference keynotes, etc.)  
  
I think this is a clue that OpenAI is likely working on solutions to reduce biased sources influencing the results (e.g. self-promotional listicles) and trying to refine the candidate pool of retrieved sources to more trustworthy, reputable information.   
  
Google also announced a core update on Friday, and I'll be curious to see what steps they are taking (if any) to reduce a lot of the potentially spammy stuff many sites have been doing, especially with scaling AI-generated content in the SERPs.   
  
There has been a lot of attention on the quality of Google's results lately (h/t [Thomas Germain](https://www.linkedin.com/in/thomas-germain/) and others) and that often seemed to serve as an impetus for Google to make big changes to its results in past years.   
  
I think we're still in the early stages of it, but I imagine this will be a year filled with countermeasures from the search/AI companies against the massive influx of new tactics that aim to manipulate their results.



## **Post 5 — Correlation vs causation in AI search studies**

What David said below is my first thought whenever I read new studies about “what works in AI search.”  
  
The outcome almost always represents something that is also essential for SEO, and I think the more plausible explanation is that there is a strong correlation between top-performing citations in AI search and top-ranking URLs in search results.  
  
Because that’s literally how RAG works.  
  
I’m not saying these studies aren’t helpful; if anything, they help validate the things we should be focused on for overall search visibility.   
  
But I see it as the classic question of correlation vs. causation: Do LLMs actually “prefer” optimized titles, clear headings, and helpful FAQs on their own? Or are those just common features of content that ranks well, and therefore content RAG is more likely to surface in the first place?  
  
There’s probably some truth to both. Structural clarity likely does help at the synthesis stage - well-organized content is easier for an LLM to parse, extract from, and cite accurately.   
  
But I believe that’s a much smaller effect than what most of these studies imply. The retrieval layer is doing the heavy lifting, and retrieval is largely downstream of SEO.  
  
So when a study says “LLMs love keyword-optimized URLs,” I think the more accurate statement is: many pages that rank well tend to have well-optimized URLs, and pages that rank well get retrieved. The optimized URL isn’t the cause - but it correlates with the thing that actually is.
