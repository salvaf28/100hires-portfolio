## **Post 3 —** Google Search Console branded vs non-branded filter — query classification insights

Google has started rolling out quick filtering options for Branded and Non-Branded queries in Google Search Console. 

This matters because query classification has always been far more complex than the old SEO reflex of labeling everything as only “transactional” or “informational.”  

Google’s systems deal with many different query types, including: 

implicit question queries 

entity-seeking queries 

numeric queries 

syntactic queries 

navigational queries 

and many others  

Navneet Panda, who is often associated with the architecture behind the Panda updates, worked on systems that evaluate site quality partly through navigational, in other words, branded queries.  

That is one of the reasons why, since my first Topical Authority case study, I have always emphasized that 0 search volume query terms and branded queries connected to topic-related queries are directly important for proving ranking necessity to Google.  

Just yesterday, we shared an example of an EMD generating $15,000 per month with only a homepage, largely because it benefits from the siteNameFactor.  

In the same way, Google now seems to be exposing a small glimpse of its internal query classification system inside Search Console.  

This is useful because what Google considers branded vs non-branded may not always be as obvious as SEOs assume.  

For example, queries containing: 

synonym variations of brand terms 

domain name terms 

site name terms 

close brand associations  

may still be interpreted as branded by Google.  

And since domain terms, site names, and brand phrases do not always perfectly overlap, this can influence how Google classifies a query.  

Because of that, the new Branded / Non-Branded filter in GSC can become a valuable hint for understanding Google’s perspective more clearly.

## **Post 4 —** E-commerce SEO: 21% more clicks YoY — product layer optimization

While getting back to my normal schedule, I wanted to briefly share one of our e-commerce observations.  

Because of the spring and summer seasonality, I am mainly looking at the year-over-year change.  

Compared to last year, the greatest improvements came from the product layer.  

This store offers prices that are 5% to 20% lower than those of many competitors, while also fitting more products per page. Every product page includes at least one video, more product images, and fully unique visuals. Product names, prices, and images are visible above the fold on both mobile and desktop. The purchase CTA is also immediately visible, along with multiple information tabs covering key attributes.  

The informational content is also fresher and consistently links to relevant product and category pages. Whenever there is an industry-specific event or seasonal trend, new blog content is published and used to pass ranking signals to product and category URLs.  

Of course, the site still has a major weakness: Shopify-related URL bloat and the ranking signal dilution that often comes with it.  

Even so, the brand has more reviews per product and per category than many of its competitors.  

This is why I keep saying that in e-commerce SEO, rankings are often shaped first by product ranking and brand ranking, before page ranking alone. Images, names, prices, buy buttons, attribute tabs, fresh informational content, internal links, and reviews all contribute to how the product and the brand are perceived.  

The result: 

21% more clicks year over year, 

12% more impressions, 

and a 52% improvement in average position.

## **Post 5 —** Extension domain case study: 17K to 765K clicks (+4,291%)

I've shared this SaaS project before in the context of extension domains, a concept I introduced publicly during my visual semantics lecture in Vilnius.  

Total Clicks: 17.4K → 765K · +4,291.6% (+747,467) 

Total Impressions: 668K → 7.48M · +1,018.8% (+6,809,014) 

Average CTR: 2.6% → 10.2% · +292.3% (+7.60 pp) 

Average Position: 23.2 → 6 · −74.1% (−17.2 positions, page 2–3 → top of page 1)  

An extension domain is a domain that sits closer to a specific topic than the main site. It either signals topical proximity through its name or stays small enough to rank and penetrate a niche more efficiently. In this setup, one site acts as the main domain, while the extension domain references and links back to it — carrying the same algorithmic authorship rules and design system, but applied to a narrower subject.  

For the December–April timeline of the visual semantics course, these two domains will be the primary case studies.  

Visual semantics is the practice of placing complex documents onto a website by tuning the size, order, and placement of web components. As text-based classification becomes harder, Google increasingly classifies sites through layout and design signals.   

A website that doesn't verbalize its visual annotations and visualize its textual insights simply won't be considered a candidate.  

This project also leverages "free" query terms to rank for "purchase / shop / premium" intent, which ties into a separate topic: query processing.
